## 2. Analyzing Your First 2–4 Videos — Framework (no data yet)

Since you don't have uploads yet, here's the exact scorecard to run once you do. Track these per video:

| Metric | Healthy benchmark (small channel) | What it tells you |
|---|---|---|
| **CTR** | 4–10% | Title + thumbnail strength |
| **Avg view duration** | 40%+ of length | Hook + pacing |
| **Audience retention (first 30s)** | >70% still watching | Hook quality |
| **Traffic source** | Browse/Suggested growing = good | Algorithm picking you up |
| **Avg % viewed** | 50%+ on Shorts | Loop-worthiness |

**How to act on it:**
- Low CTR + good retention → your content is great, fix titles/thumbnails.
- High CTR + low retention → clickbait mismatch, fix your hook and delivery.
- Compare your 2–4 videos: whichever topic + hook held retention longest = double down on that theme for the next 10 uploads.

**10 starter recommendations for your first 10 uploads:**
1. Lead every video with the result/payoff in the first 5 seconds.
2. Put one clear keyword in the title ("youth baseball hitting drill").
3. Use a face + red arrow thumbnail; test 2 variants.
4. Keep instruction videos 6–8 min until retention proves you can go longer.
5. Post 3–5 Shorts per long video (clip the best 30 seconds).
6. End every video with one specific next-watch link.
7. Pick ONE pillar (e.g., hitting) for the first 10 videos for algorithm clarity.
8. Add captions — most parents watch muted.
9. Pin a comment asking one question to boost engagement.
10. Re-upload your weakest title after 2 weeks with a new title/thumbnail to A/B it.

## 3. Ghostwriting Script — "The #1 Hitting Stat Most Baseball Parents Ignore"

*(~750 words, conversational, retention-optimized)*

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:15]**
Your kid is hitting .300 and you think they're crushing it. But there's one number on the scoresheet that tells you way more than batting average — and 9 out of 10 baseball parents have never looked at it. Stick around, because by the end of this video you'll know exactly how to spot whether your kid is actually a good hitter… or just getting lucky.

**[INTRO — 0:15–0:45]**
What's up, baseball families. If you're new here, this channel is where we use simple stats — no fancy degree required — to help your young player actually get better instead of just guessing. Today we're talking about on-base percentage, why it beats batting average, and how to track it without a spreadsheet headache. Let's get into it.

**[MAIN POINT 1 — The problem with batting average]**
Here's the thing about batting average. It only counts hits divided by at-bats. So if your kid walks four times in a game — which means they had a great eye and got on base four times — batting average completely ignores it. According to AVG, those at-bats didn't even happen. That's crazy, right? Getting on base is the entire point of being a hitter, and the stat everyone obsesses over throws half of it in the trash.

**[MAIN POINT 2 — What OBP actually measures]**
On-base percentage fixes that. It counts hits, walks, and hit-by-pitches — every single time your kid reaches base — divided by everything they did at the plate. So a kid hitting .250 who walks a ton might have a .400 on-base percentage, and that kid is helping the team win way more than a .300 hitter who never takes a walk. College recruiters know this. Pro scouts live by it. Your 10-year-old's coach probably doesn't even track it — which is exactly your edge.

**[MAIN POINT 3 — How to track it, simply]**
Now here's where most parents tap out, because they think tracking stats means math homework. It doesn't. The formula is just: times on base, divided by plate appearances. (Show the on-screen example.) Or — honestly — let an app do it. Log each at-bat as a hit, walk, or out, and your kid's real on-base percentage updates automatically. (Quick demo of logging an at-bat and seeing OBP update.) Two taps per at-bat. That's it. No spreadsheet, no calculator.

**[STORYTELLING / EXAMPLE]**
Last season I watched two kids on the same team. One hit .310 and the parents bragged about it all year. The other hit .240 — but he walked constantly, got hit by pitches, and never gave away an at-bat. When you actually looked at on-base percentage? The .240 kid was on base 42% of the time. The .310 kid? 34%. The "worse" hitter was creating way more scoring chances. The data flipped the whole story.

**[CONCLUSION — recap]**
So here's the takeaway: batting average tells you part of the story. On-base percentage tells you the truth. Track every time your kid reaches base, watch that number, and coach the walk just as hard as the hit.

**[CALL TO ACTION]**
If you want the exact tool I use to track on-base percentage, spray charts, and pitch counts in two taps, the link's in the description. And if this changed how you'll watch your kid's next game, hit subscribe — next video I'm breaking down the spray chart and how it shows you exactly where your kid needs to improve. See you there.

## 4. Finding a Profitable Niche — Baseball Sub-Niches Ranked

Within baseball, ranked by monetization + demand for a creator with your app:

| Sub-niche | Est. RPM range | Demand | Competition | Best formats |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Baseball recruiting / showcase prep** | $12–25 | High (anxious parents, high buyer intent) | Medium | Checklists, "how to get recruited," interviews |
| **Youth hitting/pitching instruction** | $6–14 | Very high | High | Drill breakdowns, swing analysis |
| **Baseball stats & analytics for parents** | $8–18 | Growing, under-served | **Low** | Stat explainers, app demos, case studies |
| **Gear/bat/glove reviews** | $10–20 (+affiliate) | High seasonal | Medium-high | Reviews, comparisons, "best of" |
| **Travel ball / cost & lifestyle** | $7–15 | High | Low-medium | Vlogs, honest cost breakdowns |

**Recommendation:** Your strongest wedge is **"baseball stats & analytics for parents"** — low competition, decent RPM, and it's a direct funnel into your app. Anchor the channel there, use hitting instruction as the high-volume traffic driver, and recruiting content as the high-RPM monetizer.

**Sample channel ideas:** "Baseball By The Numbers," "Diamond Data Dad," "The Stat Coach," "Smart Baseball Parent."
